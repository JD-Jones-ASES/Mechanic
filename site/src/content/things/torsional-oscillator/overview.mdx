Clamp one end of a shaft, hang a disk on the other, give it a twist, and let go: it rings. The
disk winds the shaft up, the shaft winds it back, and the two trade energy back and forth at one
fixed rate — the system's **natural frequency**. This is the torsional cousin of a mass bobbing on
a spring, and it is the single most important idea in machine dynamics, because every real
driveline, crankshaft, and torsion-bar is one of these hiding in plain sight.

$$\omega_n = \sqrt{\frac{k_t}{J_d}}, \qquad k_t = \frac{G J_p}{L}, \qquad f = \frac{\omega_n}{2\pi}, \qquad T = \frac{1}{f}$$

The shaft is a torsion spring of stiffness $k_t = G J_p/L$ — literally the inverse of the twist the
[shaft-in-torsion](/Mechanic/things/torsion-shaft/) page computes, $\theta = TL/GJ_p$. The disk is
the inertia $J_d = \tfrac12 m_d R^2$ — literally what the [flywheel](/Mechanic/things/flywheel-disk/)
page computes. Bolt the two together and you get an oscillator. Three things to notice:

- **The pitch ignores how hard you ring it.** There is no amplitude $\Theta$ anywhere in $\omega_n$.
  A violent swing and a gentle one oscillate at exactly the same frequency — the *isochronism* that
  Galileo spotted in a swinging lamp and that made the pendulum clock possible. What the amplitude
  *does* set is the **stress**: the peak surface shear $\tau_{max} = G d\,\Theta/(2L)$ is directly
  proportional to $\Theta$. Ring it twice as hard and the note holds while the stress doubles. Drive
  the amplitude knob up and watch the frequency sit still while the safety factor falls.
- **The material enters only as $G/\rho$.** Expand everything and the frequency is
  $\omega_n^2 = (G/\rho)\,d^4/(16 L R^4 t_d)$ — the material shows up *only* through the ratio of
  shear modulus to density, which is the square of the shear-wave speed. That ratio barely changes
  across metals, so a steel disk-shaft and an aluminium one of the same size ring at nearly the same
  pitch (swap the material in the widget and watch the frequency hardly move). Reach for a polymer,
  with a much lower $G/\rho$, and the pitch drops hard. Strength ($\sigma_y$) decides what amplitude
  the shaft *survives*, never what note it *plays*.
- **Stiffness up, inertia down.** A fatter (the $d^4$ is brutal) or shorter shaft is a stiffer
  spring and rings higher; a bigger or heavier disk carries more inertia and rings lower. This is
  why engine flywheels are sized as much for the torsional frequencies they create as for the energy
  they store.

**One material, one simplification.** Here a single material sets *both* the shaft and the disk, so
you cannot make only the disk denser in the widget — increasing density raises $J_d$ (lowering the
pitch) and changes $G$ at the same time. The counter-intuitive "a heavier disk rings lower" story
still lives in the readouts: watch $J_d$ climb with density. Independent disk-and-shaft materials
are a Phase 3 capability (multi-material binding slots); this page states the shared-material
assumption up front rather than fake it.

**The chain teaser (coming in Phase 4).** This page is built to be *chained*. Its two governing
inputs are exactly two other pages' outputs: the shaft stiffness that
[torsion-shaft](/Mechanic/things/torsion-shaft/) embodies and the disk inertia that
[flywheel-disk](/Mechanic/things/flywheel-disk/) produces. The peak restoring torque $T_{dyn}$ it
reports back is an honest torque you could feed *into* the shaft page to size it. Wire
motor → shaft → flywheel and you have simulated a real driveline's natural frequency, every number
carrying its citation. The chain-builder that lets you draw those wires arrives in Phase 4; for now
the ports are here, waiting.

A note on units: the natural frequency $f$ is measured in hertz and the spin-like $\omega_n$ in
radians per second, and although both are "per second" the site treats them as *different quantity
kinds* — you cannot silently wire a 35 Hz ring into a port expecting a 35 rad/s spin. The factor of
$2\pi$ between them is always explicit. That distinction is exactly what the dimensional type system
exists to protect.
