A spur gear pair is the workhorse of parallel-shaft power transmission: two toothed wheels whose
pitch circles roll without slipping, trading speed for torque by the ratio of their tooth counts.
The [planetary gearset](../planetary-gearset/) folds three of these meshes into one hub; a single
external pair is the atom they are built from. Fix the module $m$ (the tooth size, in mm of pitch
diameter per tooth) and the geometry is set: pitch diameter $d = mN$, centre distance
$c = m(N_p + N_g)/2$, and gear ratio

$$i = \frac{N_g}{N_p} = \frac{\omega_p}{\omega_g}$$

Power arrives as torque on the pinion and leaves as a single **tangential tooth force** at the
pitch point. Balance moments about the pinion axis — torque against force times pitch radius — and

$$W_t = \frac{2T}{m\,N_p}$$

That same $W_t$ pushes back on the gear (action–reaction), so both members carry the identical
load. This is the [power screw](../power-screw/) trick in rotary form: a torque becomes a force at
a radius, exactly as a screw's torque becomes an axial thrust.

## The Lewis equation, and why the pinion is the weak one

Wilfred Lewis (1892) modelled a gear tooth as a **cantilever loaded at its tip**, and inscribed the
largest parabola of uniform strength inside the tooth outline. The root bending stress that falls
out is

$$\sigma_b = \frac{K_v\,W_t}{b\,m\,Y}$$

where $b$ is the face width and $Y$ is the dimensionless **Lewis form factor** — everything about
the tooth's shape, distilled to one number. $Y$ grows with the number of teeth: a 12-tooth pinion
is stubby and sharply curved ($Y = 0.245$), a 100-tooth gear is nearly a rack and far beefier
($Y = 0.447$). Because $\sigma_b \propto 1/Y$, at **identical load, module, and material the
pinion always carries the higher stress** — it has the fewer teeth, so the smaller $Y$. That is the
single most useful fact in gear design: *check the pinion; it governs.* Dial $N_p$ and $N_g$ in the
widget and watch $\mathrm{SF}_p$ sit stubbornly below $\mathrm{SF}_g$ even when both gears are the
same steel.

$Y$ is not a formula. It comes from a graphical layout of the tooth (or its digital equivalent) and
is published as a table — Shigley's Table 14-2, for 20° full-depth teeth with the load near the
tip. This page carries that table as **cited data with provenance**: the widget interpolates
linearly between the published tooth counts and **refuses** (per gear, leaving the rest standing)
outside the tabulated range of 12–400 teeth, because there is no published value to stand on. The
[/verification/](/Mechanic/verification/) page states exactly what is machine-proven here (the
interpolation, the refusal, the algebra) and what rests on citation (the tabulated numbers
themselves).

## The velocity factor

Real teeth engage a hair out of step, so the load a running mesh feels exceeds the static one. Barth's
empirical **velocity factor** scales the stress up with pitch-line velocity $V = \omega_p\,m\,N_p/2$:

$$K_v = \frac{6.1 + V}{6.1}$$

(metric, for cut or milled teeth; the $6.1$ carries units of m/s). It is a fit, not a theorem —
push $V$ high enough and a banner warns that the fit has left its cited range, the same honesty the
[belt drive](../belt-drive/) applies to its capstan speed ceiling. Torque, speed, and the tooth
bending stress it drives all live on one page here, the way the [torsion shaft](../torsion-shaft/)
ties twist to shear.
