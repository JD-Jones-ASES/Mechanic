Before gears were cheap, factories ran on leather: one steam engine, one spinning lineshaft, and
belts flapping down to every machine on the floor. Belts still drive your car's alternator, your
shop's bandsaw, and every conveyor you've ever stood next to — and the same physics, run as a
rope on a drum, is how a sailor holds a docking ship with one hand. The governing fact is an
exponential:

$$\frac{T_1 - T_c}{T_2 - T_c} = e^{\mu\theta} \qquad P = (T_1 - T_2)\,v \qquad T_c = m'v^2$$

Three things to notice, one per formula:

- **Friction compounds.** Each element of wrap multiplies the tension ratio by a little more
  than its neighbor did, because friction grows with the tension that friction has already
  built — the compound-interest condition, integrated into $e^{\mu\theta}$. The numbers get
  silly fast: at $\mu = 0.3$, one turn holds 6.6×, two turns 43×, four turns ~1900×. A capstan
  is a force amplifier with no moving parts, and the *wrap angle knob* in the widget is the
  whole machine.
- **Power is sold by the tension *drop*.** The belt pulls with $T_1 - T_2$, not with $T_1$ —
  the slack side rides along at $T_2$ ≥ its centrifugal floor, un-droppable. More wrap lets the
  drive convert more of its headroom ($1 - e^{-\mu\theta}$ of it, 61 % at a half-turn for
  $\mu = 0.3$, 98 % by two turns) — each added radian captures less than the one before, which
  is why an idler that adds wrap transforms a small drive and barely registers on one that's
  already well wrapped.
- **Speed gives, then takes away.** At fixed tension the power rises with $v$ — until the
  centrifugal slice $T_c = m'v^2$, which presses the belt onto nothing and transmits nothing,
  eats the headroom. The peak sits at $v^* = \sqrt{T_1/3m'}$ (exactly one-third of the
  allowable tension doing centripetal duty), and past the ceiling $T_c \to T_1$ the widget
  *refuses*: the belt is spending all of itself staying on its own circle. Every number on this
  page is finite there — only the validity envelope knows the state is nonsense, which is the
  point.

The *deliver* configuration runs it backwards: name the power and the speed, and the widget
reports the tension the belt must survive — then $v^*$ tells you whether you're climbing the
power curve or sliding down its far side.
