The widget's safety factor guards one thing — first yield at the wall's outer fiber, where the
hogging moment $M_A = wL^2/12$ peaks. A real fixed-fixed beam has more exits, and the most interesting
ones are specific to it being **statically indeterminate**:

- **Support settlement or rotation — the assumption that can quietly move every number.** The whole
  solution rests on two premises per wall: zero deflection and zero rotation. Let one support settle by
  $\Delta$ or one built-in end rotate slightly (a column that flexes, a connection that is not truly
  rigid) and the compatibility equations pick up those movements. The reactions redistribute, the wall
  moments can swing sharply, and the "material-blind" result stops being material-blind — now $EI$
  *does* appear, multiplied by the movement. In a determinate beam a support that moves just goes along
  for the ride; in a doubly-clamped one, support movement is a load case in its own right, and a stiff
  structure is a temperamental one.

- **Yielding at the walls, then a collapse mechanism.** Because the peak moment is at the built-in
  ends, plastic hinges form there first. Unlike a simply-supported beam, the fixed-fixed beam does not
  collapse when the ends yield — the wall hinges cap their moment and the span picks up more, until a
  *third* hinge forms at midspan and the beam becomes a mechanism. That reserve (three hinges to
  collapse, not one) is a genuine advantage of the clamped beam, and it is entirely beyond the elastic
  formulas here. The $\sigma_{max} > \sigma_y$ warning marks where they stop telling the truth.

- **Thermal stress with no room to expand.** This is the clamped beam's signature failure. Pin-ended
  beams expand freely; a beam built into rigid walls at both ends cannot. Heat it and it cannot grow,
  so it develops a compressive axial stress $\sigma = E\,\alpha\,\Delta T$ that this bending-only page
  never computes — enough to buckle rails in summer heat (the classic "sun kink") or crush a
  fixed-fixed member that a simply-supported one would have shrugged off. Fixing both ends trades
  flexibility for a thermal liability.

- **Lateral-torsional buckling and thin webs.** A tall, narrow section ($h \gg b$) can twist and flop
  sideways before it yields — a stiffness failure, not a strength one — and the high shear near the
  walls can buckle a thin web.

- **Fatigue at the fixed ends.** A fixed-fixed beam that vibrates cycles its largest stress at the
  walls, usually at a welded or bolted connection full of stress raisers. Fatigue strength sits far
  below static yield, and nothing on this page addresses cyclic life.

- **Short, deep beams and brittle materials.** For $L < 10h$ (the validity warning) shear deflection,
  neglected by Euler–Bernoulli theory, is no longer negligible. And a brittle material such as gray
  cast iron has no yield plateau — rate it against ultimate strength with a generous factor instead of
  the yield-based safety factor shown here.
