Take a beam and build **both** ends rigidly into the walls — no rotation, no deflection at either
support — then load it with a uniform load $w$. A [simply-supported beam](../simply-supported-beam/)
rests on pins that are free to rotate; a [propped cantilever](../propped-cantilever/) clamps one end
and props the other. Clamp *both* ends and you get the stiffest of the lot, and the most
indeterminate. Each wall now supplies a vertical reaction **and** a fixing moment, so there are
**four** unknown reactions — $R_A, M_A$ at the left wall, $R_B, M_B$ at the right — but statics still
offers only **two** equations. The beam is *statically indeterminate to the second degree*, and
equilibrium alone is two equations short.

This is the workhorse end condition of real structures: a beam welded to columns at both ends, a
machine bed bolted down at each end, a bridge deck continuous over rigid piers, the fixed-fixed guide
rails inside a press. Fixing the ends is what a designer does to make a member stiff.

## The missing equations are compatibility — two of them

Being indeterminate to the *second* degree means two extra equations are needed, and both come from
**geometry**. Release the right end to a plain cantilever fixed at $A$ (the primary structure). Its
free end $B$ would, under the load, both drop and rotate — but the real wall allows neither. So we
impose two conditions at $B$ at once:

- **Zero deflection:** the UDL drops $B$ by $wL^4/8EI$, the redundant force $R_B$ lifts it by
  $R_B L^3/3EI$, and the redundant couple $M_B$ contributes $M_B L^2/2EI$ — together they must cancel.
- **Zero slope:** a built-in end cannot rotate either, so the tip rotations $wL^3/6EI$, $R_B L^2/2EI$,
  and $M_B L/EI$ must also cancel.

Four equations now — two equilibrium, two compatibility — for four unknowns. By symmetry the answer
is clean:

$$ R_A = R_B = \tfrac{1}{2} w L, \qquad M_A = M_B = \tfrac{1}{12} w L^2 $$

The build does not hand you these to trust. It certifies that the four relations form a system that
is **linear in the four unknowns**, solves that $4\times4$ system **exactly** at build time, and checks
the solution back through every relation. This is the `solveLinear` capability at the top of its
range — the [propped cantilever](../propped-cantilever/) is the reference $3\times3$ case, and this
clamped beam is the four-unknown one. What the machine proves and what still rests on a book is on the
[verification page](../../verification/).

## The ends govern — and the whole beam is stiffer for it

Fixing the ends does two good things at once, both visible in the widget. First, the peak bending
moment moves to the walls and *shrinks*: the hogging wall moment $wL^2/12$ is only two-thirds of a
simply-supported beam's $wL^2/8$ under the same load, and it is exactly **twice** the sagging moment
at midspan, $wL^2/24$. Because the walls carry the larger moment, first yield happens there — which is
why the safety factor is taken at the wall's outer fiber. Second, the midspan deflection collapses to

$$ \delta_{max} = \frac{w L^4}{384\,E I} $$

— **one-fifth** of the simply-supported beam's $5wL^4/384EI$. Clamping the ends is the single most
effective thing you can do to a beam short of changing its section.

## The reactions do not care what the beam is made of

Look at the compatibility steps in the derivation below. Every deflection and slope term carries the
same flexural rigidity $EI$, and because the beam is *prismatic*, $EI$ divides out completely — the
reaction equations have no $E$ and no $I$ in them. That cancellation is **machine-checked**, and it is
the lesson: **the reactions are material-blind.** Switch the material in the widget from A36 steel to
Ti-6Al-4V and watch $R_A$, $R_B$, $M_A$, and $M_B$ **not move at all**, even as:

- **Young's modulus $E$** changes the midspan deflection $\delta_{max} = wL^4/384EI$ — titanium is
  about half as stiff ($E \approx 110$ vs $200$ GPa), so it deflects nearly twice as far.
- **Yield strength $\sigma_y$** changes the safety factor $\mathrm{SF} = \sigma_y/\sigma_{max}$.
- **Density $\rho$** changes the mass.

A redundant restraint redistributes load by **geometry**, not by material. (Make the beam
*non*-prismatic — a tapered section, or two materials spliced together — and $EI$ no longer cancels;
the reactions would then follow the stiffness distribution. That is the composite-bar story, a later
THING.)

## What governs, and the sign convention

Throughout: $x$ runs from wall $A$ to wall $B$, downward load and deflection are positive, $R_A$ and
$R_B$ are the **upward** reactions, and $M_A$, $M_B$ are the **magnitudes** of the hogging fixing
moments at the two walls. The bending moment is largest at the walls: $|M|_{max} = wL^2/12$ (hogging)
beats the sagging midspan value $wL^2/24$ by a factor of two, so the walls' outer fibers are where
first yield happens and what the safety factor guards. The midspan deflection $wL^4/384EI$ is the true
maximum, exactly at the centre by symmetry.
