The widget compares two static yield criteria on a perfect surface element. Rotating machinery
has richer ways to die:

- **Rotation turns bending into fatigue — the headline.** Hold the bending moment still and
  spin the shaft: every surface point rides $+\sigma_b \to -\sigma_b$ once per revolution while
  the torsional stress sits steady. That combination — fully-reversed bending plus steady
  torsion — is THE canonical fatigue problem (Shigley's ch. 7 exists almost entirely for it),
  with allowables far below yield once cycles reach the millions. This page's static margins
  are the first-cycle sanity check, never the life prediction.
- **Stress concentrations own the failure site.** Real shafts carry keyways, shoulder fillets,
  retaining-ring grooves, and press-fitted hubs; each multiplies the local stress by 1.5–3×.
  Combined with fatigue, the failure address is almost always a feature, not the smooth
  mid-span this model analyzes.
- **The criteria gap is small next to the load uncertainty.** Tresca vs von Mises differ ≤15 %;
  belt-tension estimates, shock factors, and misalignment loads routinely differ by more. The
  bracket the two criteria draw is tight compared to how poorly $M$ and $T$ are usually known —
  which is why service factors exist.
- **Deflection and whirl.** A shaft sized for stress can still deflect enough to misalign gear
  meshes and chew bearings — and at the speed where rotation meets the shaft's lateral natural
  frequency (critical speed), deflection grows without new load. Stiffness ($E$, absent from
  this page on purpose) and bearing spacing decide that, not strength.
- **Combined is worse than the sum suggests.** A point worth teaching: adding a modest torque
  to a bending-critical shaft barely moves $\sigma_b$ but can swing the worst-plane shear
  substantially — interaction is geometric ($\sqrt{M^2+T^2}$), so the last 20 % of torque is
  cheaper to carry than the first. Intuition trained on single loads under-predicts the pair.
