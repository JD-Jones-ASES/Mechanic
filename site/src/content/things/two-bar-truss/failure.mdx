The clean split of load between two members is an *idealization* — pin-jointed, weightless,
straight, two-force bars loaded exactly through the joint. Real trusses depart from every one of those
assumptions, and the departures are where they actually fail:

- **Compression members buckle, and buckling is sudden.** A tension member yields gracefully, gives
  warning, and holds a predictable load; a compression member can find a cheaper bent shape and go all
  at once, with no nearby equilibrium to catch it. That asymmetry is why design codes put heavier
  safety factors on compression members and why this page refuses the Euler readouts below the
  transition slenderness — where the elastic formula overpredicts a short column on the dangerous side.
  The full short-vs-slender hand-off (Johnson's parabola, tangency at $\sigma_y/2$) lives on the
  [Euler Column](../euler-column/) page.
- **The joint is the usual culprit.** Members are strong; the *connection* that gathers them — the pin,
  the gusset plate, the weld — concentrates load into a small volume and is where cracks start and bolts
  shear. This page models the members and treats the joint as an ideal frictionless pin (its detailed
  design is out of scope), but in practice a truss is only as good as its joints, and fatigue at a
  connection under repeated loading kills more members than static overload ever does.
- **A load that misses the joint centroid bends the members.** The two-force-member picture holds only
  if the resultant passes cleanly through the pin. Offset it — an eccentric bracket, a cable pulling
  off-axis — and each member carries bending on top of its axial force, deflections grow from the first
  newton with no clean bifurcation, and the [eccentric column](../eccentric-column/) secant story takes
  over. A member's own weight does the same thing in miniature, sagging it into a shallow beam.
- **Shallow trusses are a trap.** The $1/\cos\alpha$ in the member force is not a curiosity: a truss
  built close to horizontal to save headroom carries member forces many times the applied load, and its
  joint deflection diverges even faster ($1/\cos^2\alpha$). Push $\alpha$ far enough and the geometry
  itself changes appreciably under load — the small-displacement assumption fails, the linear result
  understates the deflection, and a very shallow two-bar frame can **snap through** to an inverted
  shape. That large-displacement instability is beyond the linear model here; the page warns as
  $\delta$ passes $L/10$ and refuses outright at $\alpha = 90^\circ$.
- **Rigid frames are not trusses.** If the joints resist rotation (welded, bolted moment connections)
  the members carry bending as well as axial load and the determinate force split no longer holds — the
  structure is a *frame*, closer in spirit to a [cantilever beam](../cantilever-beam/) at each joint
  than to a pin-jointed truss. Assuming pins when the joints are actually stiff underpredicts the
  bending stresses that then govern.
- **Buckling picks the weakest axis.** The round bars here dodge the issue by symmetry — every bending
  axis is alike — but a rectangular or angle section buckles about its thin direction at a load the
  in-plane picture never shows. Bracing that kills the weak-axis shape can multiply a compression
  member's capacity.
