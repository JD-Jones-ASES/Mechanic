Bending gets all the attention, but a loaded beam is also being **sheared** — the transverse force
$V$ tries to slide each horizontal layer past the one below it. That longitudinal sliding is resisted
by shear stress inside the beam, and for a beam built up from separate pieces it is exactly what the
nails, screws, or bolts have to carry. This page is the shear formula $\tau = VQ/Ib$, the parabolic
stress it predicts, and the **fastener spacing** that falls out of it — the same section whose bending
the [simply-supported beam](../simply-supported-beam/) and [cantilever beam](../cantilever-beam/)
pages handle.

$$\tau = \frac{V Q}{I\,b}, \qquad \tau_{max} = \frac{3V}{2A}\ \text{(at the neutral axis)}, \qquad q = \frac{V Q}{I} = \tau\,b$$

Here $Q$ is the **first moment**, about the neutral axis, of the part of the cross-section beyond the
cut you are checking; $I$ is the second moment of the whole section, and $b$ the width at the cut.

## The parabola, and why the middle is hottest

Bending stress is largest at the top and bottom fibers and zero at the neutral axis. Transverse shear
is the **exact opposite**: it is zero at the top and bottom surfaces — there is nothing beyond them to
shear off, so $Q = 0$ — and largest at the **neutral axis**, where $Q$ is biggest. For a rectangle the
distribution is a clean parabola, and the peak works out to

$$\tau_{max} = \frac{3V}{2A} = \tfrac{3}{2}\,\tau_{avg},$$

exactly **1.5 times** the naive "force over area" value, independent of size or load. Drive $V$ and the
section knobs in the widget and watch the parabola breathe — always peaked in the middle, always dead
at the edges. (Note how *large* the safety factor usually is: transverse shear rarely governs a slender
beam. Where it bites is short deep beams — the widget warns you there — and, always, the connections.)

## Shear flow sizes the fasteners

Multiply the shear stress by the width it crosses and you get the **shear flow** $q = \tau b = VQ/I$:
the longitudinal force *per unit length of beam* that any horizontal cut must transmit. Build a beam by
nailing two planks together along the neutral axis and the nails have to carry that flow. Each fastener,
spaced $s$ apart, resists

$$F_{fast} = q\,s,$$

which is the equation that actually sets the nail spacing in a plywood box beam or the bolt pitch in a
plate girder — tighten $s$ and each connector carries less. **Convention on this page:** $F_{fast}$ is
the force at *one* interface per spacing $s$; if you put a *row of two* paired fasteners at each station,
each carries half. (The widget evaluates $q$ at the neutral axis, its maximum — the critical joint; a
seam located up at a flange carries the smaller $q = VQ_{flange}/I$ of that flange's own first moment.)

## Three quantities, one dimension — why kinds exist

This page is the catalog's clearest illustration of **[invariant 2](/Mechanic/verification/)**. The shear
flow $q$ is measured in **newtons per metre**. So is a beam's **distributed load** $w$ (the
[simply-supported beam](../simply-supported-beam/) carries one). So is a **spring rate** $k$. All three
share the identical SI dimension vector $[\mathrm{M\,T^{-2}}]$ — and yet feeding one into another would be
physical nonsense: a shear flow is not a load you can hang on a structure, and a stiffness is neither. The
engine refuses those connections not by dimension — which would allow them — but by a separate
**`quantity_kind`** tag (`shear_flow`, `line_load`, `stiffness`). Same units, three meanings, kept apart
on purpose.

There is even a second, unrelated "shear flow" in the catalog: **Bredt's** torsional shear flow in a
closed [thin-walled tube](../thin-tube-torsion/), $q = T/2A_m$, which runs *around* a closed cell rather
than *along* a beam. Same words, different physics — the open beam here versus the closed tube there is one
of the sharpest contrasts on the site.

## A strength-only material axis

Only one material property enters, and it enters weakly: **$\sigma_y$** sets the shear-yield warning
($\tau_{max}$ vs $\sigma_y/2$, the Tresca criterion) and the safety factor. Stiffness $E$ and density
$\rho$ **do not appear at all** — the elastic shear-stress distribution in a homogeneous prismatic beam is
pure statics and geometry. That is the honest picture, not a padded one: swapping steel for aluminium
changes nothing here except the margin. The [/verification/](/Mechanic/verification/) page lists exactly
what is machine-proven (the $VQ/Ib$ derivation from slice equilibrium, the $3/2$ peak, and the theorem that
the parabola integrates back to precisely $V$) and what rests on citation.
