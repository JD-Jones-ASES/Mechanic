The widget's two safety factors guard one thing each — first shear yield at the surface of each
segment, where $\tau = Tr/J$ peaks. A real doubly-restrained shaft has more exits, and the most
interesting ones are specific to it being **statically indeterminate**:

- **A support that rotates — the assumption that can quietly move every number.** The whole solution
  rests on one premise: both walls are perfectly rigid, so the end rotations are exactly zero. Let one
  wall give a little — a hub that slips on its key, a foundation that yields in torsion by an angle
  $\Delta$ — and the compatibility equation changes from $T_A a/GJ = T_B b/GJ$ to one carrying
  $\Delta$. The torque split redistributes, and the "material-blind" result stops being material-blind:
  now $GJ$ *does* appear, multiplied by the give. In a determinate shaft a support that rotates just
  goes along for the ride; in an indeterminate one, support movement is a load case in its own right.

- **Yielding in the shorter segment first, then redistribution.** Because the shorter segment carries
  the larger reaction torque, its surface yields first. Past that point the shaft does not fail
  outright — the yielded segment sheds stiffness and the split shifts toward the other wall, a
  redistribution the elastic formulas here cannot see. The $\tau > \sigma_y/2$ warning marks where they
  stop telling the truth.

- **Stress concentration at the walls and the load point.** The torsion formula assumes a smooth
  prismatic shaft. Real fixed ends are splines, keyways, or shrink-fit hubs, and the applied torque
  enters through a gear or coupling — every one of these is a stress raiser that local shear can exceed
  the nominal $Tr/J$ by a large factor. Fatigue cracks in torsion shafts almost always start at such a
  feature, not in the smooth bore.

- **Buckling of a thin-walled shaft.** If the section is a thin tube rather than a solid bar, torsional
  shear can buckle the wall into a diagonal wrinkle pattern well before the material yields — a
  stability failure this solid-shaft page does not model.

- **Fatigue.** A shaft that reverses or pulses its torque cycles the surface shear at the wall of the
  shorter segment and at every stress raiser. Fatigue strength in shear sits far below static shear
  yield, and nothing on this page addresses cyclic life.

- **Brittle materials fail differently.** The $\sigma_y/2$ shear-yield criterion assumes a ductile
  metal. A brittle shaft (gray cast iron, a ceramic) fails on the maximum *tensile* stress, which in
  pure torsion acts on a 45° helix — which is why a twisted piece of chalk breaks along a slanted
  spiral, not straight across. Rate such a material against its tensile strength, not the shear-yield
  factor shown here.
