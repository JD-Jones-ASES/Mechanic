Take a solid circular shaft, build it rigidly into a wall at **both** ends, and apply a torque $T$
somewhere along its length. A [shaft fixed at only one end](../torsion-shaft/) is *statically
determinate*: the single wall reaction must equal the applied torque, and you are done. Anchor the
second end and the problem changes character. Now there are **two** reaction torques — $T_A$ at the
left wall, $T_B$ at the right — but torsional equilibrium gives only **one** equation,
$T_A + T_B = T$. The shaft is *statically indeterminate to the first degree*, and equilibrium alone
cannot say how the applied torque splits between the two walls.

You meet this arrangement wherever a rotating member is restrained at both ends and driven in the
middle: a splined shaft keyed to fixed hubs at each end, a torsion-bar suspension anchored to the
frame at both ends and loaded by the arm, a fixed-fixed structural member picking up an applied
twisting couple.

## The missing equation is compatibility

The second equation comes not from force balance but from **geometry**. The shaft is one continuous
piece, so the rotation at the load point must be a single number. Reckoned from the left, that point
has twisted by $T_A a / GJ$ — the left segment of length $a$ carries the reaction $T_A$. Reckoned
from the right, it has twisted by $T_B b / GJ$, with $b$ the right-segment length. They describe the
same rotation, so they must be equal:

$$ \frac{T_A\,a}{GJ} = \frac{T_B\,b}{GJ} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad T_A\,a = T_B\,b $$

Combine that with equilibrium (and $L = a + b$) and the split is fixed:

$$ T_A = T\,\frac{b}{L}, \qquad T_B = T\,\frac{a}{L} $$

The build does not hand you these formulas to trust. It certifies that the two relations (one
equilibrium, one compatibility) form a system that is **linear in the two unknowns**, solves that
$2\times2$ system **exactly** at build time, and checks the solution back through both relations — the
same total verification an ordinary closed form gets. This is the `solveLinear` capability; the
[propped cantilever](../propped-cantilever/) is its reference beam case, and this shaft is its
smallest — a genuinely coupled two-unknown solve. What the machine proves and what still rests on a
book is spelled out on the [verification page](../../verification/).

## The larger reaction lands on the shorter segment

Read the result again: $T_A = T\,b/L$. The **left** wall's share is set by the length of the
**right** segment. Slide the load toward the left wall — small $a$, large $b$ — and $T_A$ grows toward
the full torque $T$ while $T_B$ fades. The near wall does the heavy lifting; the short, stiff segment
between the load and the near wall is harder to twist, so it carries more torque. Because each segment
carries its own reaction, each has its own surface shear stress $\tau = Tr/J$, and the shorter segment
is where the higher stress — and first yield — appears. That is why the widget reports $\tau_1$ and
$\tau_2$ separately, with a safety factor for each.

## The reaction torques do not care what the shaft is made of

Look at the compatibility step in the derivation below. Both twists carry the same torsional rigidity
$GJ$, and because the shaft is *prismatic* — uniform shear modulus and uniform radius — $GJ$ divides
out completely. The line $T_A a = T_B b$ has no $G$ and no $r$ in it. That cancellation is
**machine-checked**, and it is the lesson: **the reaction torques are material-blind, and radius-blind
too.** Switch the material in the widget from A36 steel to 6061 aluminium and watch $T_A$, $T_B$,
$\tau_1$, and $\tau_2$ **not move at all**, even as:

- **Shear modulus $G$** changes the twist $\varphi = T_A a / GJ$ — aluminium is about a third as stiff
  in shear ($G \approx 26$ vs $79$ GPa), so the same load twists it nearly three times as far.
- **Yield strength $\sigma_y$** changes the safety factors $\mathrm{SF} = (\sigma_y/2)/\tau$.
- **Density $\rho$** changes the mass.

A redundant restraint redistributes torque by **geometry**, not by material — the "sharing" between
the two walls is set purely by the segment lengths. (Make the shaft *non*-prismatic — a stepped shaft,
two different diameters spliced at the load point — and $GJ$ no longer cancels; the split would then
follow the stiffness distribution. That is a later story.)

## What governs, and the sign convention

Throughout: $x$ runs from the left wall $A$ to the right wall $B$, the applied torque $T$ acts at
$x = a$, the segment lengths are $a$ and $b = L - a$, and $T_A$, $T_B$ are the reaction-torque
**magnitudes** at the two walls (their senses oppose the applied torque). The shear stresses
$\tau_1 = T_A r/J$ and $\tau_2 = T_B r/J$ grow linearly from zero at the axis to their peak at the
surface, exactly as in the [simple torsion shaft](../torsion-shaft/); the shorter segment carries the
larger of the two. Shear yield is taken at $\sigma_y/2$ by the maximum-shear-stress criterion, the
same convention the torsion-shaft page uses.
