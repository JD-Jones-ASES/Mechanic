A slider-crank, like its [four-bar](../fourbar-linkage/) parent, mostly *misbehaves* kinematically and
dynamically long before a link breaks — and several of its troubles are written directly into the
obliquity angle $\phi$ this page computes.

- **Side thrust and bore wear.** The connecting-rod force $F/\cos\phi$ is never aligned with the
  cylinder; the leftover $F\tan\phi$ presses the piston sideways against the bore, reversing direction
  each stroke. That is the "piston slap" that wears cylinder walls oval and the reason engines keep
  $l/r$ generous — a longer rod means a smaller $\phi$ and less side thrust. Drive $r/l$ past about
  $0.5$ on this page and the warning fires for exactly this reason.
- **Secondary imbalance.** The exact acceleration carries a twice-per-revolution term — the
  $\frac{r}{l}\cos 2\theta$ piece of $a\approx -r\omega^2(\cos\theta+\frac{r}{l}\cos 2\theta)$. A rotating
  counterweight on the crank can cancel the once-per-rev shaking force, but nothing rotating at crank
  speed cancels a force at *twice* crank speed. This "secondary" shake is why inline-four engines carry
  balance shafts spinning at $2\omega$, and it grows with $r/l$.
- **Dead centres.** At top and bottom dead centre the crank torque $T=Fr\sin(\theta+\phi)/\cos\phi$ is
  zero: the gas force points straight through the crank axis with no moment arm. An engine coasts through
  on flywheel inertia, but a mechanism that must *start* against load from a dead centre cannot — the
  same toggle geometry that locking pliers exploit on purpose.

## The torque ripple, and why the flywheel exists

The single clearest lesson of the force pass is that crank torque is not constant. Over one revolution
$T(\theta)$ swings from zero at a dead centre to a mid-stroke peak and back — and in a real engine it
goes *negative* over the compression stroke, when the crank drives the piston rather than the other way
round. A load, meanwhile, wants steady torque. The mismatch between the engine's lumpy delivery and the
load's steady demand is stored and released twice a revolution by a [flywheel](../flywheel-disk/): its
rotational inertia soaks up the excess near the torque peak and gives it back through the dead centres,
holding the speed fluctuation to a chosen coefficient.

Sizing that flywheel means **integrating** the torque curve — accumulating the energy $\int(T-T_\text{mean})\,\mathrm{d}\theta$
between the speed extremes to get the required inertia. This page refuses to do that honestly: it
evaluates $T$ *at* the crank angle you set, exactly, but it does not integrate over the cycle, because
time- and angle-integration of a varying quantity is a dynamics capability the catalog has deliberately
not built yet. So the flywheel story is told here and *sized* on the [flywheel](../flywheel-disk/) page —
the torque ripple is the input that page's energy method consumes, and wiring one THING's output into the
next is the job of the chaining work still to come. Naming the hand-off rather than faking the integral
is the honest version.

## Where material finally re-enters

Everything on this page is geometry and quasi-static force, so nothing here depends on what the parts are
made of. The failures that *do* depend on material live one link away:

- **Crankshaft torsional vibration.** The crankshaft is not rigid — it is a torsional spring carrying
  disks (the cranks and flywheel), and the very torque ripple above feeds it at multiples of crank speed.
  Hit a torsional natural frequency and the shaft winds and unwinds enough to fail in fatigue; big
  engines fit tuned dampers for precisely this. That is the [torsional oscillator](../torsional-oscillator/)
  story, and its stiffness and inertia carry $G$ and $\rho$.
- **Rod buckling and bearing loads.** The connecting rod is a column under the compressive force
  $F/\cos\phi$ computed here, alternating with tension — a fatigue-and-buckling problem the
  [combined bending + torsion shaft](../combined-shaft/) and column pages own. The rod force and its
  obliquity are the loads those analyses start from.

So the slider-crank's own page stops at the clean, material-blind skeleton: where the piston is, how fast
it moves, and what torque a given gas force makes. Every failure that needs a modulus, a density, or a
yield strength is deliberately handed to the THING that carries it.
