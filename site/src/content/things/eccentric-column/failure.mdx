The secant formula is exact elastic theory for an idealized member — and each idealization marks
a way the real column quits earlier:

- **First yield is the beginning of the end, not a nuisance.** Past $P_y$ the yielded fiber stops
  taking extra stress, the effective section shrinks, the bow deepens, and the moment arm grows —
  the same amplification loop that built the secant formula now feeds on a *softening* column.
  Collapse follows at a load not far above first yield, which is why $P_y$ (and not some plastic
  reserve) is the design ceiling this page reports. The widget refuses the elastic readouts past
  first yield rather than pretend the fiction continues; $P_y$ and $\mathrm{SF}_P$ stay exact.
- **The margin must be taken on load.** A stress-based factor of safety silently assumes
  proportionality. Here a "factor of 2 on stress" can evaporate into far less than a factor of 2
  on load — the page shows both margins so the gap is impossible to miss. Codes write this rule
  down (apply the factor to the load, then check the stress); Shigley states it as the reason the
  secant equation must be solved iteratively at all.
- **Approaching $P_E$ is hopeless even though nothing has "buckled."** The deflection diverges
  asymptotically; stiffness against the bow goes to zero. Real eccentric columns are serviceable
  only well below the Euler load — by the time $P$ reaches even $0.7\,P_E$, the amplification
  factor is brutal and growing without bound. There is no event to wait for, just runaway.
- **Both-ends-same-side bowing is the worst case this page models.** Equal eccentricities on the
  same side give single-curvature bending with the peak at midspan. Opposite-side offsets
  (reverse curvature) are gentler; a single-end offset lands between. The pinned, equal-offset
  case is the standard conservative chart.
- **Tension can appear on the convex face.** Push the eccentricity ratio up and the bending
  gradient overwhelms the uniform compression — the neutral axis walks into the section and part
  of it goes into tension (watch the zero-stress line enter the bar in the sim). Bolted bases,
  grouted joints and masonry — things that carry compression but not tension — fail this way
  long before any fiber yields.
- **Crooked columns are eccentric columns in disguise.** An initial bow $a$ behaves nearly like
  an end eccentricity; design codes fold real-world crookedness, residual stress and accidental
  offset into equivalent-imperfection rules precisely because the perfectly straight,
  perfectly centered member of the [Euler page](../euler-column/) does not exist in any shop.
