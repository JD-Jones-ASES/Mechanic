The [thick-walled cylinder](../thick-walled-cylinder/) ends on a refusal: the bore shear always
exceeds the pressure, so past $p = \sigma_y/2$ **no amount of wall** contains the pressure
elastically — the widget there diverges and says so. This page is the classical cheat. Machine
the liner's outside a few **microns** larger than the jacket's inside, heat the jacket, drop it
on, let it cool. The interference $\delta$ generates a contact pressure at the interface:

$$p_c = \frac{E\,\delta}{r_c}\,\frac{(r_c^2 - r_i^2)\,(r_o^2 - r_c^2)}{2\,r_c^2\,(r_o^2 - r_i^2)}$$

and that contact pressure squeezes the liner bore into **hoop compression** —
$-2 p_c r_c^2/(r_c^2 - r_i^2)$, about $-69$ MPa at this page's defaults — parked exactly where
service pressure will later hurt most. Pressurize the assembly and (same metal throughout) the
wall responds as one monobloc cylinder with the frozen assembly field riding on top: the bore's
tension must first *spend* the compression before it can start doing damage.

Nothing is free — the relief the liner enjoys is tension the jacket prepaid at the interface.
The page therefore carries **two margins**, and the knobs slide the danger between them:

- **Under-shrunk: the bore governs.** Small $\delta$ leaves the wall nearly monobloc;
  $\mathrm{SF}_i$ is the binding margin, $\mathrm{SF}_c$ loafs.
- **Over-shrunk: the interface governs.** Every micron of interference lowers the bore shear
  and raises the interface shear. Push far enough and the bore stays in net compression at full
  pressure — at which point a *tensile* bore margin is meaningless, and the widget **refuses
  that one readout** (the jacket margin and the assembly-compression warning carry the story
  instead). Keep pushing and assembly alone yields something — before any pressure exists.
- **Balanced: equal margins.** The two shears are linear in $p_c$, one falling, one rising, so
  they cross exactly once; the $\delta_{bal}$ readout names that crossing for the current
  pressure. The defaults sit exactly on it: $\mathrm{SF}_i = \mathrm{SF}_c = 1.39$ at 100 MPa —
  a pressure that the *same envelope as a one-piece wall* would barely survive at
  $\mathrm{SF} \approx 1.0$.

The headline is the ceiling. Balance the fit, put the interface at the geometric mean
$r_c = \sqrt{r_i\,r_o}$ (the defaults do: $\sqrt{40 \times 90} = 60$ mm), and the elastic
capacity becomes $\sigma_y\,(1 - r_i/r_o)$ — against the monobloc's
$(\sigma_y/2)(1 - r_i^2/r_o^2)$, a gain of $2/(1 + r_i/r_o)$: **two shells approach twice the
ceiling** as walls grow thick. Both the optimum location and the doubling are machine-proven in
the test pipeline, not quoted. This is 19th-century gun-barrel arithmetic, and it is why
built-up construction exists.

Two quiet notes worth knowing. First, there is no Poisson knob on this page: with both members
the same metal, $\nu$ **cancels exactly** from the closure — the derivation below proves the
cancellation with $\nu$ left symbolic (different metals would keep all four of
$E_i, \nu_i, E_o, \nu_o$). Second, the shrink fit is secretly a *chaining* story: the fit turns
microns into a pressure, $\delta \mapsto p_c$, and that pressure is exactly the kind of typed
port — pascals, `pressure_stress` — that another THING's bore could someday consume. The
[rotating disk](../rotating-disk-bore/) left its bore free for precisely this reason.
